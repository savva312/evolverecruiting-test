## 2) Nigeria demand and personas

- **SSCE/WAEC and A‑level high achievers** – strong Biology/Chemistry students who narrowly miss places at Nigerian federal universities or UK medical schools and want an EU‑based, English‑taught MD.
- **Middle‑ and upper‑income families targeting “global” medicine** – comfortable paying high five‑figure USD/EUR totals over six years if the pathway looks credible for practice in Nigeria, the UK, US, or Gulf.
- **Students already abroad (Caribbean/CEE/TRNC)** – transfer‑minded candidates who want more structured clinical placements, stronger student support, or clearer post‑graduate mobility.

What usually matters most:

- **Total cost of study** (tuition + living + travel) benchmarked against UK, CEE, and Caribbean options.
- **Clinical exposure quality** – teaching hospitals, early patient contact, supervision standards, and where rotations actually take place.
- **Licensure and mobility** – realistic routes to practice in Nigeria and other countries (steps, exams, supervised practice).
- **Safety and community** – perceived safety in Cyprus/Greece, Nigerian student community, and pastoral/visa support.

## 3) Positioning vs competitors

- **EU‑based, English‑taught MD** – position UNIC as an EU medical degree with English‑medium teaching and structured clinical placements, compared with TRNC and some Caribbean options whose regulatory status feels less stable to families.
- **TRNC affordability vs EU stability** – acknowledge that some Northern Cyprus (TRNC) universities advertise significantly lower net tuition because of large “automatic” scholarship packages, but emphasise that families should prioritise accreditation quality, clinical‑placement standards, and long‑term mobility over the lowest sticker price; use the TRNC entries in the competitors page as concrete examples.
- **Admissions route** – holistic assessment (grades plus interview) rather than heavy entrance‑exam culture common in CEE publics; attractive for WAEC/A‑level students strong in coursework but anxious about high‑stakes tests.
- **Affordability vs UK / high‑cost destinations** – use sourced data to show that, while medicine is always expensive, UNIC’s MD tuition and Nicosia/Athens living costs typically sit well below UK MBBS fees for international students and below many North American or Caribbean options.
- **Student experience and support** – multicultural cohorts, structured academic support, and scholarship schemes (for example, need‑based discounts up to around 20% of tuition) that can soften the affordability story for Nigerian families.

Use the competitors file for concrete university examples and numbers when preparing counselling decks, webinars, and agent training.

## 4) Nigeria buyer journey

- **Discovery:** agents, school counsellors, alumni, and social media content about “Study Medicine in Europe”.
- **Shortlisting:** families compare UNIC to Cyprus/Greece/CEE/Caribbean providers recommended by agents; they ask for tuition tables, payment plans, and recognition statements.
- **Validation:** parents seek reassurance from Nigerian doctors abroad, MDCN contacts, or WhatsApp groups; unclear answers about licensure or clinical placements are a major drop‑off risk.
- **Decision and onboarding:** timing is constrained by WAEC/NECO results, UK offer/rejection timelines, and visa appointment pressure; clear next‑step checklists and pre‑departure webinars reduce melt.

## 5) Messaging pillars for Nigeria

- **Serious clinical training, not just theory** – highlight hospital partners, supervised patient contact from early years (where accurate), and support for key licensing exams (for example, USMLE/PLAB‑style preparation).
- **Transparent cost story vs UK and others** – show side‑by‑side costs using sourced tuition figures and realistic living‑cost bands; avoid saying medicine is “cheap” and instead frame UNIC as high‑quality at a lower total cost than many UK or Caribbean routes.
- **Safe, supported landing for Nigerians** – emphasise accommodation options, local mentors, Nigerian student groups, and access to staff who understand Nigerian exam systems and visa processes.
- **Multiple future pathways, no guaranteed outcomes** – describe typical post‑MD routes (postgraduate training in EU/UK/US, return to Nigeria after supervised practice and required exams) while stressing that outcomes depend on individual performance and regulator decisions.

## 6) Licensure and recognition cautions (Nigeria)

When discussing recognition, NYSC, licensure, or practice rights:

- **Never promise automatic recognition** in Nigeria or any other country, and do not imply that an EU MD guarantees residency or specialty training.
- Clarify the difference between:
  - **Academic recognition** of the degree (for example, by ministries of education or WES‑style evaluators), and
  - **Professional licensure** (for example, approval by the **Medical and Dental Council of Nigeria (MDCN)** or other national councils).
- Encourage families to check current rules directly with MDCN, the Federal Ministry of Education, and destination‑country regulators.

Always route counsellors back to the [Nigeria recognition and licensure guide](../../market/recognition-and-licensure.md) for:

- A Nigeria‑specific explanation of recognition versus licensure.
- Links to Nigerian authorities and professional councils.
- Medicine‑specific pathway notes and last‑verified dates.

Keep all wording conservative and factual, avoid legal advice, and resist pressure from agents or families to make specific licensure or residency guarantees.
