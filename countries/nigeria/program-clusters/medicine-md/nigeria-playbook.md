# Nigeria playbook — Medicine / MD

## 1) Core personas and objections

- **SSCE/WAEC and A‑level science high‑achievers**
  - *Motivations:* become doctors, ideally in Nigeria or the UK; want serious clinical training and clear post‑graduate options.
  - *Top objections/questions:*
    - “Will this degree be recognised by MDCN and allow me to practise in Nigeria?”
    - “Is the total cost affordable compared with UK or local private options?”
    - “How safe are Cyprus and Greece for Nigerian students?”

- **Families considering CEE/Caribbean via agents**
  - *Motivations:* a credible MD with lower admission barriers; comfort in having an agent “handle everything”.
  - *Top objections/questions:*
    - “Agents say Bulgaria/Hungary/Caribbean are cheaper and ‘guarantee’ placement—why UNIC instead?”
    - “Can my child transfer from a CEE or Caribbean school into UNIC?”

- **Graduates / transfers from other medical tracks**
  - *Motivations:* move from a weaker school or programme into a better‑structured MD with more predictable outcomes.
  - *Top objections/questions:*
    - “Will my previous credits count?”
    - “Will switching delay my graduation too much or hurt my licensure plans?”

## 2) Common objections and suggested responses

Use these as internal prompts; always adapt to the student’s profile and avoid legal or licensure promises.

- **“Is the MD recognised in Nigeria?”**
  - Acknowledge that UNIC’s MD is an accredited EU medical degree and is listed in major global directories, but **professional licensure decisions sit with regulators** like the Medical and Dental Council of Nigeria (MDCN).
  - Point families to `countries/nigeria/market/recognition-and-licensure.md` for current Nigeria‑specific guidance and official links.
  - Emphasise that graduates typically complete additional steps (exams, supervised practice) before full registration anywhere, including Nigeria.

- **“Where will my clinical placements be, and are they guaranteed?”**
  - Explain that clinical training is organised through affiliated hospitals in Cyprus and Greece, and—for a subset of students—through international partner sites, all subject to capacity and regulatory approvals.
  - Avoid guarantees about a specific hospital, country, or speciality placement; frame placements as **structured but not individually guaranteed**.

- **“How does cost compare with the UK?”**
  - Use sourced tuition data to show that UK MBBS programmes frequently charge **£40,000–£60,000 per year** in tuition alone for international students, while UNIC MD tuition is in the low‑ to high‑€20,000s per year, with lower typical living costs than London or other major UK cities.
  - Share realistic total‑cost ranges (tuition + living + flights) and highlight scholarships or payment plans, but avoid saying medicine is “cheap”.

- **“Will my child match into residency abroad?”**
  - Emphasise that outcomes depend on academic performance, exam scores, and visa/country rules; UNIC provides support (for example, exam preparation and career advising) but **cannot guarantee any specific residency match**.
  - Encourage families to see the MD as opening **pathways** rather than guaranteeing a specific destination.

## 3) Channel strategy

### 3.1 Schools and counsellors

- Prioritise high‑achieving Nigerian secondary schools with strong science tracks, IB/A‑level provision, or a history of medicine placements.
- Tactics:
  - Cluster‑specific school talks: “Studying Medicine in Cyprus and Greece – routes from WAEC and A‑levels”.
  - Small‑group counsellor briefings focused on licensure, costs, and scholarship rules rather than broad marketing.
  - Co‑host sessions with Nigerian UNIC medical students or recent graduates who can answer practical questions.
- Data capture:
  - Maintain a simple school‑level log of medicine‑interested students (graduation year, counsellor contact, stage in decision).

### 3.2 Agents and partners

- Prioritise agencies already strong in medicine placements for EU/CEE and track their historical conversion and compliance.
- Tactics:
  - Provide a **cluster‑specific agent toolkit**: factsheet on UNIC MD, comparison one‑pager vs CEE/Caribbean, licensure guardrails, and sample talk tracks.
  - Run mandatory virtual training sessions where agents must acknowledge licensure/clinical‑placement disclaimers and agree not to over‑promise outcomes.
  - Build incentives around **quality and compliance**, not just volume (e.g., recognition for agents whose students remain in good standing and progress on time).

### 3.3 Digital channels

- Search / Performance:
  - Campaign themes such as “Study Medicine in Europe (Cyprus & Greece)” and “Alternative to UK Medicine – EU MD options”.
  - Target keywords around “MBBS in Cyprus/Greece from Nigeria”, “MD in Europe for Nigerians”, and “medicine abroad after WAEC”.
- Social (Meta/TikTok/YouTube):
  - Short‑form student‑story content focusing on first‑year experience, clinical skills labs, and daily life in Nicosia/Athens.
  - Live Q&A sessions with admissions and current Nigerian students around WAEC and UCAS timelines.
- Nurture:
  - Email / WhatsApp sequences that drip content: recognition FAQs, cost breakdowns, scholarship guidance, and webinar invites.

## 4) Webinar and activation concepts

- **“From WAEC to White Coat: Medicine in Cyprus and Greece for Nigerian Students”**
  - Audience: WAEC/NECO and A‑level students plus parents.
  - Content: admissions routes, overview of UNIC Nicosia and UNIC Athens, affordability vs UK, and licensure guardrails.
  - Speakers: Nigerian current students, faculty member, admissions counsellor.

- **“Medicine Pathways and Licensure for Nigerian Families”**
  - Audience: parents and counsellors who are already comparing CEE/Caribbean/UK routes.
  - Content: difference between academic recognition and professional licensure, common myths, where to find authoritative information.
  - Always signpost back to the Nigeria recognition and licensure doc and regulator links.

- **Offer‑holder prep sessions**
  - Practical sessions on visa steps, budgeting, what to pack, and how to succeed academically in the first year, ideally co‑hosted with Nigerian student leaders.

## 5) Affordability framing

- Lead with **total cost of study**, not only tuition:
  - Combine tuition, mandatory fees, estimated living costs, flights, and exam/visa expenses into ranges.
  - Provide side‑by‑side comparisons vs selected UK, CEE, and Caribbean competitors using sourced fee data.
- Emphasise **value and predictability**:
  - Stable published tuition for the MD, with clear information on malpractice and clinical‑supplement fees.
  - Scholarship and bursary options (for example, need‑based reductions up to around 20% of tuition), but never imply that a scholarship is guaranteed.
- Avoid problematic language:
  - Do not describe medicine as “cheap” or promise that a student will be able to “earn it back quickly”.
  - Keep messages anchored in affordability relative to other high‑quality options.

## 6) Licensure, recognition, and clinical placements — compliance language

Use or adapt the following internal boilerplate across schools, agents, and digital content:

- **On recognition and licensure**
  - “UNIC’s MD programmes in Nicosia and Athens are fully accredited medical degrees in their host countries. However, decisions about where graduates can practise medicine are made by national regulators (for example, the Medical and Dental Council of Nigeria and equivalent bodies abroad). Graduates typically complete additional steps—such as national licensing exams and supervised training—before full registration. For Nigeria‑specific guidance and links to official regulators, see `countries/nigeria/market/recognition-and-licensure.md`.”

- **On clinical placements**
  - “Clinical placements are organised through approved teaching hospitals in Cyprus, Greece, and selected international sites. Placements are subject to availability, partner‑hospital capacity, and regulatory approvals. UNIC cannot guarantee a specific hospital, country, or speciality for any individual student, but it does commit to providing supervised clinical training that meets programme accreditation standards.”

- **On outcomes**
  - “The MD degree opens pathways toward postgraduate training in many countries, but residency and specialist training places are competitive and never guaranteed. Outcomes depend on each graduate’s performance, exam scores, language skills, and immigration rules.”

Every school visit, agent meeting, or webinar that touches medicine must:

- Use cautious, sourced language on recognition and licensure.
- Refer back to the Nigeria recognition and licensure materials.
- Log any new licensure questions that cannot be answered with current sources so they can be fed into future recognition‑and‑licensure updates.
