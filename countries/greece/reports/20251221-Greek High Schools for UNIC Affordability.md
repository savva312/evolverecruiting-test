# Greek High Schools for UNIC Affordability

## Produced by: UNIC Evolve, December 20, 2025

# 1) Executive Summary

This report identifies, city-by-city, the Greek upper-secondary schools most likely to enroll students from households that can plausibly afford UNIC tuition at the levels you specified: \$10,000 per year at UNIC Nicosia (and \$22,000 for Medicine), or \$15,000 per year at UNIC Athens (and \$27,000 for Medicine). The practical implication is that the strongest “ability-to-pay” signals in Greece are concentrated in a small number of school types and geographies: premium international/IB schools and high-fee private school networks, overwhelmingly in Athens/Attica, and secondarily in Thessaloniki.

## What matters most for your objective

- **Athens/Attica is the primary high-probability market.** It contains multiple international and premium private schools with published upper-secondary fees in the €13,000 – €16,000 range, and established international university pathways (IB DP, US high school diploma options). These are the clearest “families can pay \$10,000 – \$15,000 per year for university” feeders, and the most credible source of Medicine candidates at \$22,000 – \$27,000 per year because the households already budget at a comparable annual level for schooling.

- **Thessaloniki is the second high-probability market.** It has a smaller but meaningful set of premium options, led by an international school with published fees around €13,750 for upper secondary and additional boarding packages. This is the strongest non-Attica concentration of families who already pay in the “UNIC tuition band” for secondary education.

- **Outside Athens and Thessaloniki, “premium-fee” signals are rare.** Most qualifying municipalities (population ≥ 50,000) either:
  - have **a small number of private Greek Lyceums** (often 1–3) with fees commonly **not published** (and when published, frequently in the mid-single-thousands of euros), or
  - have **no private upper-secondary units at all** in the municipality, which shifts outreach strategy toward: (a) commuter-market private schools in the same prefecture, (b) tutoring ecosystems, or (c) boarding-capable schools in Athens/Thessaloniki.

- **Implication for Medicine outreach:** your most efficient “medicine tuition” funnel is likely to come from the **highest-fee international/IB schools** (Athens/Attica, Thessaloniki), plus selected “international-pathway” private schools in other regions where there is evidence of dual diploma, foreign diploma tracks, or strong overseas counseling. In many other municipalities, the private-school base exists but may not be “expensive enough” to be a reliable medicine-tuition proxy without additional screening.

## City coverage: what “fully comprehensive” means in this report

- The “cities of 50,000+” requirement is operationalized as **municipalities (Δήμοι) with ELSTAT 2021 permanent population ≥ 50,000**, which yields **64 municipalities nationally**.
- A majority of these 64 are **within Attica**, meaning many “cities” by threshold are Athens-area municipalities that share a single integrated school market. The report therefore treats **Athens/Attica as one core market** (while still acknowledging the municipality threshold universe), because premium schools recruit region-wide.
- For every non-Attica, non-Thessaloniki municipality in the ≥ 50,000 universe, the report will either list the private/international upper-secondary schools located in the municipality or state explicitly that **no private upper-secondary units are present** (and, where operationally helpful, indicate nearest commuter-market private options).

## Highest-priority outreach targets (shortlist view)

The report will ultimately provide full, sourced school tables; the list below is a decision-oriented preview of the **highest probability** feeder environments based on two signals: (1) **published high upper-secondary fees** and/or (2) **international upper-secondary pathways** (especially IB DP, US diploma, British senior school).

| Priority tier | Where to focus first | Why it is most decision-relevant for UNIC affordability |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1: “Direct affordability match” | **Athens/Attica premium international and IB schools** (examples include ACS Athens, International School of Athens, Athens College IB track, Pierce IB, Costeas-Geitonas IB) | Published upper-secondary fees commonly fall in the €13,000 – €16,000 range, a close proxy for capacity to pay \$10,000 – \$15,000 per year at university; these schools also normalize international degree pathways. |
| Tier 1: “Direct affordability match” | **Thessaloniki premium international/boarding schools** (notably Pinewood; plus Anatolia’s IB and boarding ecosystem) | Published fees and boarding costs indicate a comparable annual spending capacity and strong outbound university intent, including Medicine prospects. |
| Tier 2: “International-pathway without transparent fees” | Select Athens/Thessaloniki international schools with programs verified but fees not publicly posted (for example, long-established IB schools) | The pathway signal is strong, but tuition confirmation requires direct outreach to validate the “expensive enough” criterion. |
| Tier 3: “Private-pay, local-market” | Regional private Greek Lyceums in qualifying municipalities outside the metros (for example, in Patras, Larissa, Volos, Ioannina, Heraklion, Rhodes, Kalamata, Chania) | These schools confirm a fee-paying segment; however, many publish no fees or publish at levels below the “UNIC band,” so student-level screening and counseling partnerships become more important. |
| Tier 4: “No private upper-secondary units locally” | Municipalities ≥ 50,000 with no private Lyceum/EPAL units in the municipality | Outreach should pivot to commuter-market private options within the prefecture, tutoring centers, and digital campaigns targeted by income/profession rather than by school. |

## Bottom line

If your goal is a practical, high-yield target list of Greek high schools whose families can pay UNIC tuition at \$10,000 – \$15,000 per year (and \$22,000 – \$27,000 for Medicine), the evidence strongly supports a two-speed strategy:

1. **Concentrate the majority of outreach effort on Athens/Attica and Thessaloniki**, where premium-fee international/IB schools provide the clearest, repeatable affordability proxy and the strongest international degree orientation.

2. **Maintain comprehensive national coverage across all ≥ 50,000 municipalities**, but treat most regional cities as lower-density markets where private-school presence is thinner, fees are less transparent, and conversion depends on targeted counseling relationships and screening rather than high-fee tuition alone.

# 2) Scope, Definitions, and Affordability Logic

## 2.1 What this report is (and is not)

**Purpose:** identify Greek upper-secondary schools, in every Greek municipality with permanent population ≥ 50,000, that are the best outreach targets for students whose families can *plausibly* afford the stated UNIC tuition levels:

- UNIC Nicosia: \\\$10,000 per year (Medicine: \\\$22,000)
- UNIC Athens: \\\$15,000 per year (Medicine: \\\$27,000)

**What the list represents:** a school-based “addressable market” view, not a promise that every student at each school can pay. The goal is to locate school communities where there is a credible concentration of households with the capacity and the orientation (international degree intent) to pay these tuition levels.

**What the list does not attempt:** household income verification, exact family affordability rates by school, or complete coverage of tutoring centers (φροντιστήρια) and other non-school channels, unless explicitly used as a “nearest alternative channel” when no private upper-secondary schools exist in a municipality.

## 2.2 Definition of “city of 50,000 on up to Athens”

To make “fully comprehensive” operational and auditable, the report treats “city” as a **municipality (Δήμος)**, using **ELSTAT Census 2021 permanent population (μόνιμος πληθυσμός)** and including every municipality with **≥ 50,000** permanent residents.

Key implications:

- This produces a **finite national universe (64 municipalities)** that the report must cover, even when the finding for a municipality is “no private upper-secondary units located here.”
- Many qualifying “cities” are **within Attica**, meaning they share a single integrated school market. The report therefore treats **Athens/Attica as one metro school market** for targeting purposes, while still respecting the municipality threshold universe for completeness.
- The report similarly treats **Thessaloniki Regional Unit (Π.Ε. Θεσσαλονίκης)** as the Thessaloniki metro school market, because private schools recruit across the metro.

## 2.3 What counts as “high school” (upper secondary) in this report

The report focuses on **upper secondary education**, meaning schools that deliver the final secondary credentialing stage.

Included as “upper secondary”:

- **Greek General Lyceum** (Γενικό Λύκειο, often shown as private **Ιδιωτικό ΓΕΛ** or **Ιδιωτικό Λύκειο**), typically A’, B’, C’ Lyceum (Grades 10–12 equivalent).
- **Private Vocational Lyceum** (Ιδιωτικό ΕΠΑ.Λ / EPAL) when it is a fee-paying upper-secondary unit.
- **International upper secondary equivalents** delivered by fee-paying schools, for example:
  - IB Diploma Programme (IB DP)
  - British senior school years (IGCSE/A-Levels or equivalent)
  - American high school diploma (sometimes alongside AP)
  - Foreign national pathways (for example, French lycée / German international diplomas), where offered as part of a fee-paying school.

Explicit exclusions:

- **Public schools** (tuition-free) are not listed as “expensive enough” targets, even though some may serve affluent catchments.
- **Tutoring/cram schools** (φροντιστήρια), language schools, test-prep centers.
- **Post-secondary institutes** (IEK) and tertiary “colleges” unless they clearly operate a secondary upper-secondary unit.
- **Schools that stop at Gymnasium** (lower secondary) or primary only, unless called out separately for transparency.

## 2.4 What “expensive enough” means (affordability proxy logic)

Because Greek schools generally do not publish family income profiles, “expensive enough” is implemented as a **proxy** based on observable school characteristics that correlate strongly with higher household ability to pay international university tuition.

### Core affordability signals used

The report prioritizes schools that show one or more of the following signals:

1. **Published high annual fees for upper secondary**
   - Strongest single proxy: published fees for Grades 10–12 or equivalent around **€10,000+ per year**, especially €13,000 – €16,000 (common among premium international schools in Athens/Attica and the leading international school in Thessaloniki).
   - When fees are lower (for example, €5,000 – €7,000), the school still indicates a fee-paying segment, but it is a weaker direct proxy for the UNIC tuition bands.

2. **International credential pathways**
   - IB DP, US diploma tracks, British senior school, foreign national diplomas.
   - These pathways indicate both ability to pay and intent to study abroad, which materially increases UNIC relevance.

3. **Premium positioning and services**
   - University counseling infrastructure, international admissions orientation, boarding options, and English-medium delivery.

4. **Affluent catchment as a secondary signal**
   - Used cautiously, mainly within large metro areas where a school’s municipality and brand are reliably associated with higher-income families.
   - This never substitutes for evidence of upper-secondary provision.

### How UNIC tuition bands are interpreted

- The report uses UNIC tuition levels as an **ability-to-pay benchmark** and recognizes that tuition is only one component of total cost (living, travel, books, fees).
- Currency conversions (EUR to USD) fluctuate; the report therefore does not treat any single exchange rate as definitive. Instead, it relies on fee “order of magnitude” and pathway signals.

### How fee transparency is handled

- If a school publishes fee schedules, those are reported with the stated academic year.
- If fees are not published, the report marks them as **Not published** and relies on program and positioning signals, without inventing numbers.
- Third-party fee estimates are used only when clearly labeled as non-official and are not treated as a substitute for direct confirmation.

## 2.5 Evidence standard and what “comprehensive” means at the school level

To support a “fully comprehensive” claim under the municipality definition above, the report uses two complementary verification approaches:

- **Greek private upper-secondary units:** confirmed via the national school unit registry (sch.gr) as private (ΙΔΙΩΤΙΚΟ) and as an upper-secondary unit type (ΓΕΛ or ΕΠΑΛ), then assigned to municipalities based on the unit’s **Δήμος** field (not branding or marketing language).
- **International pathway claims:** verified through authoritative program registries where possible (for example, IB World School listings) and the school’s official program pages.

This approach ensures that each qualifying municipality is treated consistently: either it contains verified fee-paying upper-secondary school units (listed), or it is explicitly documented as having none (with outreach implications addressed in the action sections of the report).

Athens/Attica is the highest-yield recruitment market in Greece for your stated affordability benchmarks (\$10,000 – \$15,000 per year, and \$22,000 – \$27,000 per year for Medicine). The reason is structural: Attica concentrates (a) the largest number of **fee-paying upper-secondary schools**, and (b) the highest density of **international pathways** (especially IB DP and American or British upper secondary), which correlate strongly with both ability-to-pay and intent to pursue private university options.

# 7) Outreach Prioritization and Next-Step Playbook (UNIC Recruitment Use Case)

This section translates the school and city inventory into an outreach plan optimized for your central constraint: identifying students whose households can plausibly afford **UNIC tuition of \\\$10,000 – \\\$15,000 per year** (and **Medicine at \\\$22,000 – \\\$27,000 per year**), using school-based signals of ability-to-pay and international study intent.

## 7.1 Targeting tiers (what to prioritize first)

### Tier 1: “Direct affordability match” (highest yield per contact)
**Definition:** Schools with published upper-secondary fees approximately **€13,000+**, or premium international schools where high fees are clearly established and international progression is standard.

**Primary markets:** Athens/Attica, then Thessaloniki.  
**Why it works:** Families already budget at a level that resembles your target tuition, so affordability friction is reduced, including for Medicine.

**Operational goal:** Establish formal counselor relationships and recurring annual pipeline activities (presentations, parent evenings, alumni panels).

### Tier 2: “International pathway, fees not public”
**Definition:** Schools with verified IB DP or other international upper-secondary pathways, but tuition is not transparently published.

**Why it works:** Intent to study abroad is strong, but you must qualify affordability during counselor conversations.

**Operational goal:** Get program-level confirmation (student profile, typical overseas destinations, scholarship prevalence) and capture tuition reality through counselor or admissions contacts.

### Tier 3: “Private-pay local Lyceums (non-metro)”
**Definition:** Private Greek Lyceums outside the two metros, often the only 1–3 fee-paying upper-secondary options in their municipality.

**Why it can still work:** Even when school fees are modest, these schools can include a meaningful subset of households willing to fund private university education, especially for professional programs.

**Operational goal:** Focus on student-level screening and on “decision accelerators” (clear pathways, outcomes, and cost planning), rather than assuming broad affordability.

### Tier 4: “Municipalities with no private upper-secondary units”
**Definition:** A municipality meets the ≥ 50,000 threshold but has no confirmed private ΓΕΛ or private ΕΠΑΛ units located within its boundaries (as listed in the report’s city tables).

**What to do instead:**  
- Identify **nearest commuter-market private options** within the same prefecture and treat them as the “school-based proxy” targets for the city.  
- Run a **non-school channel plan** (Section 7.4) in parallel.

## 7.2 Recommended sequencing (90-day launch plan)

### Weeks 1–2: Build the target account list and contact map
1. **Start with Tier 1 in Athens/Attica and Thessaloniki** (premium international and high-fee IB schools first).  
2. For each school, create a contact record for:
   - Head of Upper School / Lyceum Director
   - University and Careers Counselor (or “College Counseling” office)
   - Admissions Director (international programs often sit here)

**Minimum CRM fields to capture per school:**
- Programs offered: Greek Lyceum, IB DP, US diploma, British, French, German
- Published fees (year) and whether “Not published”
- Typical graduate destinations (UK, NL, CY, etc., as reported by the counselor)
- Languages used for counseling and parent communication
- Prior engagement history, if any
- Best annual timing: university fairs, parent nights, application season

### Weeks 3–6: Secure counselor alignment, then present to families
- Run short, counselor-first calls (20–30 minutes): confirm student profile, affordability cues, and calendar fit.
- Convert to one of three commitment types:
  1. **On-campus talk** (Grade 11–12, or IB DP Year 1–2)
  2. **Parent webinar** (evening, bilingual if needed)
  3. **1:1 counselor toolkit distribution** (digital pack)

### Weeks 7–12: Expand beyond Tier 1, and launch regional coverage
- Expand to Tier 2 (IB/international pathway, fees not public) and Tier 3 (regional private Lyceums in ≥ 50,000 municipalities).
- For Tier 4 municipalities, activate commuter-market schools plus non-school channels.

## 7.3 Messaging pillars (what to say, tailored to affordability and fit)

Use a consistent set of messages that match the realities of Greek decision-making: outcomes, safety of plan, and clarity of cost.

### Core (all programs)
- **Value clarity:** transparent tuition structure and what is included.
- **Academic pathway fit:** how students transition from Greek Lyceum or IB into UNIC academic expectations.
- **Career relevance:** employability outcomes, internships, and clear progression routes.
- **Practicality:** location, student support, language support (where applicable), housing guidance.

### Medicine-specific (high tuition, higher scrutiny)
- **Cost planning:** acknowledge openly that Medicine tuition is higher (\\\$\*22,000 – \\\$27,000 per year) and emphasize predictable budgeting and support.
- **Readiness alignment:** clarify entry requirements, language requirements, and expected academic preparation (without overselling).
- **Family reassurance:** highlight student support structures and the long-run plan (study habits, counseling, advising).

### “Greek private Lyceum” segment (Tier 3)
- Emphasize **step-by-step pathway** and the “safe alternative plan” for families seeking private university options without uncertainty.
- Offer **counselor tools**: admissions timeline, checklist, and a clear FAQ on costs beyond tuition.

## 7.4 Channel plan for “no private upper-secondary units” municipalities

When a qualifying municipality has no private upper secondary schools, school-based outreach will underperform unless supplemented. A practical channel mix:

1. **Commuter-market schools in the prefecture:** treat them as the proxy school targets for the municipality’s affluent segment.  
2. **Tutoring ecosystem partnerships (φροντιστήρια):** focus on those that advertise overseas admissions support, English proficiency pathways, or SAT/UK admissions support.  
3. **Professional community targeting:** medical, dental, engineering, shipping (where relevant), and entrepreneurial associations often correlate with ability-to-pay for private education.  
4. **Digital campaigns by geography and interest:** parent-focused campaigns emphasizing affordability planning and international pathway clarity, targeted to the municipality and adjacent commuter zones.  
5. **Alumni and referral strategy:** parents trust outcomes, so alumni voices (especially in health fields) can shift interest quickly.

## 7.5 Rapid qualification checklist (confirm “expensive enough” and intent within 1–2 interactions)

Use this as a standard script for counselor calls and for school discovery when fees are not public.

### Affordability cues (school-level)
- Does the school publish fees for upper secondary, and are they in a premium band?
- If not published: is there evidence of a large fee-paying base (private Lyceum status, premium positioning, high-cost services such as boarding)?
- What portion of graduates pursue paid private universities abroad?

### Intent cues (student-level, via counselors)
- Is the student pursuing international applications (IB, UK, NL, US, etc.)?
- Family preference: public university in Greece versus private abroad?
- Language readiness: English proficiency sufficient for English-medium programs?

### Practical conversion cues
- Who drives the decision: student, parent, both?
- Timing: when does the family finalize the plan (Grade 11 versus Grade 12)?
- Sensitivity: is the family comparing tuition only, or total cost of attendance?

## 7.6 What “success” looks like (KPIs)

To manage this as a pipeline rather than a one-off campaign:

- **Tier 1 engagement:** number of counselor relationships activated, and number of confirmed annual events on the school calendar.
- **Lead quality:** percent of leads meeting an affordability threshold (self-reported or inferred from school tier and counselor feedback).
- **Medicine conversion funnel:** inquiry to application conversion rate, and yield by school tier.
- **Regional coverage:** number of ≥ 50,000 municipalities with at least one active channel (school-based or alternative).
