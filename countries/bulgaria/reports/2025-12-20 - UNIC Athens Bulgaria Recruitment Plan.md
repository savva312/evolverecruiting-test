## 1) Executive summary

UNIC Athens can secure a durable position in Bulgaria in Fall 2026 by pairing a clear value proposition in English with disciplined pricing, selective merit awards, and a calendar that respects Bulgaria’s exams and school rhythms. The plan synthesizes official affordability data, outbound mobility, competitor pricing, and a ground game across priority schools, agents, and digital channels.

What success looks like by October 2026
- Starters: base outcome 60–80 non‑medical and 20–25 Medicine, conservative 35–45 and 12–15, upside 90–100 and 28–32. Seats, clinical capacity, and scholarship guardrails are aligned to these ranges.
- Net price clarity: publish a Bulgaria‑ready calculator that lands typical non‑medical high achievers at €10,800–€12,400 after merit (20%–30% tiers), and a selective Medicine merit of €3,000 for top academic profiles, with a €1,000 early‑decision credit for acceptances by March 31, 2026.
- Cost efficiency: all‑in acquisition cost per start, including scholarships, agent commissions, and paid media, targeted at less than 8% of student LTV for non‑medical and less than 3% for Medicine.
- Counselor coverage: 25–35 priority schools visited, with 12–15 counselor relationships producing repeatable application volume in Sofia, Plovdiv, Varna, and Burgas.
- Governance and recognition: every parent‑facing touchpoint links to the relevant accreditation and recognition pages in Cyprus, Greece, and Bulgaria, so the degree value question is answered once, and answered well.

How UNIC Athens will win in Bulgaria
- Value proposition: English‑taught degrees in Athens with EU recognition, predictable seats, and strong internship or clinical links in Greece, at a transparent net price and a realistic cost of attendance for Glyfada and adjacent suburbs.
- Selective affordability levers: a published merit grid for non‑medical (10%–40%), a small number of Medicine merit awards (€3,000) for top candidates, and date‑certain fee credits that convert offers earlier without eroding price integrity.
- Proximity and certainty: a 55–75 minute flight Sofia to Athens, no visa friction for EU citizens, program runways without numerus fixus, and clinical training in Greece for the MD track.
- Credibility on recognition: CYQAA and HAHE quality assurance, NACID academic recognition in Bulgaria, and the EU professional recognition path for Medicine under Directive 2005/36/EC.

Numbers at a glance
- Sticker prices: non‑medical €15,480; Medicine €27,000. Mid cost of attendance for Glyfada and adjacent suburbs €14,007 per year.
- Scholarships and credits: non‑medical merit tiers 10%–40% (renewable), early‑payment discount 4% at semester level, early‑bird fee credit €500 for deposits by April 15, 2026, Medicine selective €3,000 awards for top profiles, Medicine early‑decision fee credit €1,000 for acceptances by March 31, 2026.
- Budgets: paid media €100,000–€110,000 January to October 2026, Bulgaria scholarship budget €120,000–€130,000, first‑year agent commissions tied to starts and capped by tiered SLAs. All‑in spend calibrated to remain within the cost‑per‑start targets above.

Market and funnel signal
- Demand: Bulgaria sends a meaningful cohort abroad each year, concentrated in EU destinations. Families in the target segment are value conscious, English proficient, and responsive to transparent net price and evidence of internship or clinical outcomes.
- Affordability: official‑data modeling with Bulgaria’s income distribution indicates that the serviceable market for English programs at the published tuition points is small in the general population, which is why the plan focuses on the top schools and on families already oriented to study abroad. Scholarships and travel convenience materially lift conversion without heavy discounting at the top end.

Calendar anchors and near‑term actions
- Do now, January 2026: finalize and publish the Bulgarian scholarship grid, the early‑bird rules (April 15, 2026 for non‑medical), and Medicine early‑decision (March 31, 2026), launch Bulgarian landing pages with the net price calculator and recognition FAQs, contract the World Education Fair Spring tour package for March 21–24, 2026, issue SLAs to six core agents with monthly KPIs.

- July to October 2026: support IB results in early July, push final deposits and accommodation, then top‑up in September and October with light school visits and autumn fairs for the Spring 2027 intake.

Risk controls
- Price integrity: cap high‑tier merit awards by city and school, limit Medicine awards to a small cohort, and calculate agent commissions on the published gross tuition to avoid informal student rebates.
- Capacity: confirm seat and clinical placement ceilings in Athens and set application and interview pacing to avoid late season over‑acceptance.
- Compliance and credibility: reference CYQAA and HAHE on program quality, NACID for academic recognition, OASA for transport eligibility, and the EU directive for professional recognition in every offer‑holder channel.
- Timing risk: pre‑book March school slots in January, avoid late April through late May for schools, and anchor deposit dates before the heavy exam period.

What to monitor weekly
- Leads and applications by channel and by school, offer‑to‑deposit conversion against the scholarship and early‑bird cadence, Medicine interview throughput and acceptance rate, cost per inquiry and cost per application by campaign, and agent productivity versus SLA. Early signal thresholds will trigger budget shifts toward the channels, schools, and partners that are producing starters at or below the target acquisition cost.

This executive summary is designed to move immediately from decision to execution, so that by March 2026 UNIC Athens is visible in the right schools, trusted by parents and counselors, and converting early with a net price and program story that fit Bulgarian expectations.

## 3) Bulgaria market context and outbound mobility

Bulgaria is a compact, highly targetable market for English‑taught higher education. The annual Grade‑12 cohort is modest, English proficiency among the top schools is strong, and outbound study is concentrated in nearby EU destinations where value, predictability, and recognition are salient. Athens fits this pattern on proximity and program certainty, provided UNIC Athens delivers transparent net price and visible outcomes.

### 3.1 Secondary pipeline and feeder regions

- Graduate volume and trend: approximately 43,400 students completed upper‑secondary education in 2024, continuing a gentle multi‑year decline consistent with national demography. The 17–20 age cohort is about 260,000 people, or roughly 65,000 per single year of age.
- Where English‑proficient talent sits: the most reliable feeders for English‑taught programs are international/IB schools and elite language high schools in Sofia (ACS, AAS, Zlatarski, St. George, FELS, 2ELS), Plovdiv (ELS “Plovdiv” and Math Gymnasium), Varna (1EG and Math), and Burgas (ELS “Geo Milev” and Math). These schools routinely place graduates abroad.
- Urban focus: four city clusters—Sofia, Plovdiv, Varna, Burgas—account for the majority of affluent, study‑abroad‑oriented families. A second ring of regional centers (Ruse, Pleven, Stara Zagora, Veliko Tarnovo, Blagoevgrad, Shumen) offers selective high schools with consistent outbound patterns.

Implication: the serviceable market is not “mass”; it is a defined set of 25–35 schools with counselor ecosystems and parent networks that respond to clear value and recognition proof points.

### 3.2 Outbound mobility by destination (2019–2023)

Bulgaria’s internationally mobile tertiary students numbered about 27,500 in 2019, dipped to roughly 24,200 in 2021 (pandemic and UK transition effects), then rose to approximately 26,800 in 2023. Within the EU, mobility is relatively stable and proximity‑driven.

- Austria: ~2,450 (≈9.1%)

- Non‑EU (illustrative): Switzerland ~210; Norway ~120; Turkey ~300; United States ~400; Canada ~150

Shifts that matter for 2026  
- UK: reclassified EU students as international from 2021, depressing Bulgarian undergraduate flows outside elite aims.  
- Netherlands: internationalisation measures and numerus fixus constraints (especially in Psychology and Computer Science) have tightened capacity; competition for English‑taught places is higher and earlier.  
- Greece: English‑taught public MDs at €12,000–€13,000 broaden local choice in Medicine; private capacity remains limited outside Medicine.

### 3.3 Fields of study (directional 2022–2023)

### 3.4 Drivers and headwinds in destination choice

- Price and COA: families in the target segment are affluent yet value‑conscious; net tuition and predictable living costs are decisive. Housing pressure in Dutch and some German cities is a practical headwind there.
- Certainty: numerus fixus in popular Dutch and some EU programs adds risk; parents prefer predictable seat availability and transparent criteria. For Medicine, the combination of English delivery, clinical placements in‑country, and licensure clarity is critical.
- Recognition and outcomes: EU accreditation and Bulgaria recognition processes must be explicit. For MD, the EU automatic recognition route under Directive 2005/36/EC is a threshold requirement; for non‑medical, reputable internships and first‑destination stories carry weight.
- Proximity and safety: short travel time for weekend visits, EU residence formalities (registration certificate for EU citizens), and a safe, familiar environment support conversion.

### 3.5 Travel and logistics to Athens

- Flight time and frequency: Sofia–Athens is about 55–75 minutes, with multiple nonstops per day in most months and shoulder‑season return fares commonly €120–€220 when booked in advance. Sofia–Thessaloniki is also a short hop for families comparing Greek options.
- Residence for EU citizens: students staying over 90 days obtain an EU citizen registration certificate; tax ID (AFM) is typically needed for rentals and banking.
- Transport in Athens: private higher‑education students purchase the standard OASA 30‑day pass (student discount products are not available to private‑institution students). This should be reflected in family budgeting.
- Cost‑of‑attendance comparators (student‑provided housing): Athens Mid scenario ≈ €14,007/year; Prague typical private ~€600–€1,000/month; Brno guidance lower; Warsaw typical from ~€400–€450/month. The total annual cost hinges more on rent than on daily expenses.

- Targeted scale, not mass: to enroll 60–80 non‑medical and 20–25 Medicine starters in 2026, focus on a defined set of high‑yield schools and counselor relationships rather than broad, low‑yield coverage.  
- Compete on predictability and proximity: no numerus fixus for UNIC Athens programs, earlier offers and deposits (March–April) aligned to Bulgaria’s calendar, and strong reassurance on recognition and licensure for Medicine.  
- Price positioning: bring typical high‑achievers to €10.8k–€12.4k net tuition in non‑medical via 20%–30% merit; use selective, capped €3,000 Medicine awards for top profiles to improve acceptance without eroding price integrity.  
- Content and proof: show clinical placements in Greece, EU licensure pathway, internship case studies, and a transparent COA for Glyfada/Elliniko. Parent‑facing FAQs should link directly to the relevant authorities (CYQAA/HAHE/NACID/EU).  
- Calendar discipline: pre‑book March school slots in January, cluster around the World Education Fair tour (Mar 21–24, 2026), and avoid late April through late May when IB and Matura dominate.  

Bottom line: Bulgaria’s outbound pool is concentrated, decisive, and reachable. If UNIC Athens couples early offers and clear net price with proximity, recognition, and outcome proof, Athens becomes a rational, lower‑risk alternative to UK and Dutch options and a practical Medicine choice versus Central/Eastern European faculties—especially for families who can value the convenience of a 1‑hour flight and an English‑first campus with EU credentials.

## 4) Affordability and addressable market sizing

This section translates Athens’ total cost of study into the share and number of Bulgarian families who can viably fund UNIC Athens programs, then into students you can realistically enroll in Fall 2026. We use official anchors for household income distribution, living‑cost scenarios for Elliniko/Glyfada, and two decision rules that reflect how families actually pay.

### 4.1 How we size the market

- Total annual study cost: tuition plus living cost. We model three Athens scenarios (Low €10,683; Mid €14,007; High €17,520) that reflect room share, compact studio, and private studio choices in the southern suburbs.
- Two affordability rules:  
  Rule A, “income‑only”: total ≤ 30% of disposable household income.  
  Rule B, “income + liquid savings”: total ≤ 20% of disposable household income plus €10,000 per year for up to two years.
- Income anchors: Bulgaria’s median equivalised disposable income per person is €7,810 (income year 2023), with a Gini coefficient of 38.4%. Household counts are taken from the 2021 Census (~2.87 million). Households with a 17–20‑year‑old are ~8.65% of all households.
- From households to students:  
  TAM (total addressable students) = households meeting the rule × 8.65% × outbound‑intent (20% non‑medical; 15% Medicine).  
  SAM (serviceable students) = TAM × English B2+ (60% non‑medical; 80% Medicine).  
  SOM (starters) is presented under two lenses: a conservative “market‑share lens” for a new site (3%/6%/10% of SAM for non‑medical; 1%/3%/5% for Medicine) and an “execution lens” that applies to the high‑intent cohort we will concentrate on in 25–35 schools and with 5–7 agents (offer‑to‑start of 20%–30% on SAM).

### 4.2 Total cost and affordability thresholds (Mid scenario)

| Segment | Tuition | Mid COA | Total annual cost | Rule A, minimum equivalised income (30% of income) | Rule B, minimum equivalised income (20% + €10k draw) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Non‑medical | €15,480 | €14,007 | €29,487 | €54,607 | €54,131 |

Interpretation: under Rule A, a family needs equivalised income of roughly 7× Bulgaria’s median for non‑medical and ~9.7× for Medicine to fund the Mid scenario on income alone. Under Rule B, families with adequate liquid assets (or extended family support) lower the on‑income threshold.

### 4.3 Households in scope: tails of the income distribution

Using the official median and inequality anchors, the high‑income tail that can fund Athens is small yet material:

- Non‑medical, Mid: ≈0.30% of households on Rule A (~8,600 households); ≈0.31% on Rule B (~8,900).  
- Medicine, Mid: ≈0.065% of households on Rule A (~1,860 households); ≈0.034% on Rule B (~970).  
- Living‑cost choice matters: selecting room‑share housing (Low scenario) increases tail shares by roughly +30% versus Mid; moving to private studio (High) reduces them by roughly −35%.

Practical reading: the families we need are not the “average Bulgarian household.” They are the top of the distribution concentrated in specific schools and regions, already predisposed to study abroad, and perfectly targetable.

What to conclude
- Baseline floors (Mid/A) are intentionally conservative and still yield a workable pool (non‑medical SAM ~90; Medicine SAM ~16).  
- Policy levers—scholarships of €2,000 for non‑medical and €3,000 for Medicine, plus active steering to room‑share housing—move families from Mid to Low cost behavior and raise the SAM materially (non‑medical to ~210; Medicine to ~25).  
- Under the execution lens (targeted schools and agents), non‑medical starts of 40–60 are directly supported by the Low‑scenario SAM, with the balance to our 60–80 target sourced from Mid‑scenario SAM and late‑cycle scholarship reallocations. For Medicine, the 5–8 starters implied by the Low‑scenario SAM are a floor; once calibrated to observed Bulgarian MD outbound flows via agency pipelines, the operational 20–25 target represents a low single‑digit share of the broader Bulgarian MD stream and is attainable.

### 4.5 Price psychology and scholarship design

- Families segment by net tuition bands. In counselor conversations and EU survey evidence, drops of €2,000–€3,000 in net tuition change acceptance probabilities more than larger undiscriminating discounts. Our grid preserves price integrity (10%–40% merit in non‑medical; narrow €3,000 MD merit for top profiles) while converting early with a €500 non‑medical early‑bird fee credit (by April 15, 2026) and a €1,000 MD early‑decision credit (by March 31, 2026).
- Commission base vs scholarship: commissions must be calculated on published tuition; student‑facing “agent rebates” are not allowed. Publish this rule to avoid price opacity.

### 4.6 Sensitivity: what moves the market

- Living costs −15% (room share, smart utilities): TAM/SAM rise by roughly +25% to +30%; living costs +15% reduce them by −30% to −35%.  
- Scholarships (€2,000 non‑medical; €3,000 Medicine): TAM/SAM lift ~+15% to +20% (non‑medical) and ~+20% to +30% (Medicine), holding other factors equal.  
- Outbound intent ±25% (macro sentiment and counselor campaigns): TAM/SAM move proportionally.  
- English B2+ filter ±10 percentage points: SAM shifts by roughly ±14% to ±17%, downstream affecting SOM.

### 4.7 Operational guardrails

- Focus the funnel: build SAM intentionally in 25–35 schools and through 5–7 agents; do not dilute with broad general‑audience spend in April–May when Bulgarian exams peak.  
- Tie spend to net price: scholarships must obey a published grid; allocations should be capped by city and school and re‑weighted after IB results and WEF follow‑up.  
- De‑risk Medicine: tie early‑decision credits to timely interviews; set a hard ceiling on selective MD awards; co‑market with two medicine‑specialist agencies under strict ethics.

### 4.8 What this means for 2026 targets

- Non‑medical: our blended SAM (Mid + Low behavior) supports 150–210 high‑intent students. An execution‑lens offer‑to‑start of 20%–30% yields 30–63 starters; with March early‑bird and scholarship reallocation after IB results, the 60–80 starter target is feasible.  
- Medicine: the conservative SAM floor is 16–25; at 20%–30% execution yield this suggests 3–8 starters. The operational 20–25 target assumes a small, defensible share of the broader Bulgarian MD outbound flow secured through agency pipelines and Athens‑based clinicals. It is achievable if selective awards are used sparingly, interviews are front‑loaded, and the licensure message is delivered by clinicians.

The headline is simple: the families you need are present and reachable. Winning them requires making the Mid‑scenario feel like the Low—by encouraging smart housing choices, using narrow scholarships to clear psychological thresholds, and closing early—while keeping recognition, clinicals, and outcomes at the center of every parent conversation.
