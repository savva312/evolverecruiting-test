# Affordability and economics

## Affordability signals for Bulgarian students
- Families expect EU-fee treatment and look for clear scholarship grids; 10–30% merit discounts are seen as standard while deep full-ride offers are rare.
- Upfront cost control matters because Cypriot visas require proof of funds; early-payment discounts and predictable installment plans reduce proof-of-funds pressure.
- Living-cost sensitivity is high: Athens is typically framed as slightly lower cost-of-living than Nicosia, while both remain cheaper than Netherlands/UK tiers.
- Erasmus+ mobility stipends (≈€450–600/month) are familiar and used as a budgeting offset for a semester abroad rather than core degree financing.

## Financing norms
- Bulgarian state-backed student loans mainly target domestic study; for overseas degrees students rely on consumer loans from DSK/OTP and family support.
- Parents value the ability to stack modest scholarships (merit + family + early payment) rather than a single large award; clarity on caps/stacking is required.
- Payment schedules aligned with seasonal income (summer work, remittances) help reduce churn risk after deposit.

## Positioning implications for UNIC vs UNIC Athens
- Lead with transparent merit bands (10–30% EU-student relief) and pre-approve early-payment discounts to lower visa proof-of-funds amounts.
- Bundle family/sibling discounts for small cohorts from the same Bulgarian school/agency to win group decisions without steep one-off awards.
- Highlight Athens when cost-of-living is a blocker, and Nicosia when students value a more established campus; keep scholarship messaging identical to avoid channel confusion.
