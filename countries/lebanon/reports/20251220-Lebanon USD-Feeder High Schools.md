# Lebanon USD-Feeder High Schools

## Produced by: UNIC Evolve, December 20, 2025

# 1) Executive Summary: Where to Find Lebanese High-School Families Who Can Sustain \$10,000 – \$15,000+ Annual University Tuition

Lebanon’s currency devaluation changed what “expensive school” means. Nominal tuition in Lebanese pounds (LBP) is no longer a reliable indicator of purchasing power. The strongest, observable proxy for a family’s ability to sustain international university tuition (such as UNIC Nicosia at about \$10,000 per year, UNIC Athens at about \$15,000 per year, and materially higher medicine tuition) is whether a school requires **recurring, material USD payments** for the secondary cycle, commonly labeled as **support funds, solidarity funds, operating fees, development funds, ancillary fees, or USD tuition lines**.

This report therefore targets schools whose fee structures demonstrate that families can consistently access and spend USD at scale, which is the closest practical analogue to paying overseas tuition.

## 1.1 Decision-relevant conclusions (what matters most)

### A) “Expensive enough” in Lebanon (planning definition)
For recruitment purposes, the most defensible indicator of ability-to-pay is:

- **Verified secondary cycle** (Grades 10–12, Seconde–Terminale, 1AS–3AS, and/or IB DP1–DP2), **and**
- A **required annual USD component** for secondary, evidenced publicly (official fee PDF/page/image on the school’s domain or an official file host clearly linked by the school).

Practically, schools fall into three affordability tiers:

- **Tier 1: Strong ability-to-pay signal (best match for \$15,000+ and medicine targeting)**  
  Schools with **very large annual USD obligations** and/or predominantly USD-based billing for secondary (often international and top-tier private schools). These households are the most plausible candidates for UNIC Athens pricing and for higher-cost programs such as medicine, subject to family priorities and sibling load.

- **Tier 2: Moderate ability-to-pay signal (good match for \$10,000; selective for \$15,000+)**  
  Schools with a **clear annual USD component** in the low-to-mid thousands plus LBP tuition. These families often can fund \$10,000 per year abroad when the value proposition is strong, and may stretch to \$15,000 depending on household circumstances.

- **Tier 3: Borderline (use cautiously; prioritize scholarship pathways and financing conversations)**  
  Schools with small USD add-ons or unclear/undated fee statements. These markets can still produce qualified candidates, but conversion to full-pay international tuition is less predictable.

### B) Where the addressable volume is concentrated (highest yield cities first)
Based on where Lebanon’s most USD-intensive secondary fee structures cluster, the recruitment “center of gravity” is:

1) **Greater Beirut**  
   The densest concentration of elite private and international secondary schooling, and the highest likelihood of large annual USD fee obligations.

2) **Mount Lebanon affluent belt (the primary feeder extension of Beirut)**  
   High concentration of premium private schools and boarding options, including areas such as **Baabda corridor (Hazmieh, Jamhour, Hadath and surroundings), Metn (Antelias and nearby), and Keserwan-Jbeil (Zouk/Adonis area and surroundings)**. This zone produces substantial cohorts who are already paying significant USD-linked school costs.

3) **Selective non-Beirut markets (lower volume but still relevant where evidenced)**  
   - **Saida (Sidon)**: identifiable high school pathways including IB DP, with evidence of USD annual components in certain schools.  
   - **Multi-campus networks in the South/Nabatieh areas**: can matter operationally when the network publishes consistent USD components and when campus linkage is clear.

### C) Diminishing returns rule (how far the report should go city-wise)
After **Beirut** and the **Mount Lebanon belt**, incremental cities tend to produce either:
- few schools with publicly verifiable **annual** USD secondary requirements, or
- smaller USD add-ons that are weaker predictors of sustained \$10,000 – \$27,000 annual overseas tuition.

Accordingly, the report will include additional cities **only where the fee evidence meets the hard criteria**, then stop expanding geography once additional cities fail to add meaningful numbers of “audit-passed” schools.

## 1.2 How to use the list (what it enables immediately)
Section 2 of the report will function as a practical targeting tool:

- **City-by-city prioritization** for school visits, counselor engagement, and parent events.
- **A defensible “ability-to-pay” signal per school**, grounded in published fee structures rather than reputation alone.
- **Program-specific targeting guidance**:
  - For **UNIC Nicosia (\$10,000 per year)**: prioritize Tier 1 and Tier 2 schools, and treat Tier 3 as opportunity for merit aid and financing conversations.
  - For **UNIC Athens (\$15,000 per year)**: prioritize Tier 1 schools, plus a shortlist of Tier 2 schools where USD obligations and international pathways are clearly established.
  - For **medicine (\$22,000 – \$27,000 per year)**: concentrate on the strongest USD-fee schools, and frame outreach around outcomes, licensure pathways, and total cost planning.

## 1.3 What you should expect from the final deliverable
- A **structured, city-organized list** of qualifying high schools (and K–12 schools with an explicit secondary cycle), each with:
  - secondary-cycle confirmation,
  - the **annual USD component** (amount and label),
  - curriculum/pathway signals (IB, American/AP, French Bac, Lebanese Bac),
  - and a clear affordability fit tag aligned to the UNIC tuition benchmarks.
- A clearly separated **watchlist** of “likely elite feeders” that could not be included under the hard rules because annual USD secondary fees are not publicly posted or campus linkage cannot be proven from official sources.

  - **High:** Strong match for \$15,000+ per year pathways, and often the best match for medicine-level affordability.

# 3) Method, Inclusion Rules, Audit Trail, and Caveats (Including Devaluation and the City Stop Rule)

## 3.1 Inclusion criteria used in this report (what qualified a school)
A school appears in Section 2 only if it meets **both** requirements below:

1) **Secondary-cycle confirmation (required):** The school publicly confirms a secondary/high-school cycle using explicit labels such as:
- Grades **10–12**
- **Seconde–Première–Terminale**
- **1AS–3AS**
- **IB DP1–DP2** (or equivalent)

2) **Annual USD requirement for secondary (required):** The school publicly shows a **required annual USD component** for secondary students, such as:
- USD-denominated tuition, or
- an annual USD “support,” “solidarity,” “operating,” “development,” or “ancillary” fee/fund, or
- another clearly **annual** USD payment tied to secondary enrollment

One-time entry fees, deposits, and dossier fees were treated as **insufficient on their own** for inclusion, because they do not demonstrate sustained annual USD spending capacity.

## 3.2 Why USD-indexed school fees are the best affordability proxy after devaluation
The Lebanese context requires separating nominal pricing from real ability-to-pay. Post-devaluation, many private schools publish a mixed structure (LBP tuition plus USD-linked funds and fees). For the purpose of identifying families who can plausibly sustain **\$10,000 – \$15,000+ per year** for university (and higher for medicine), the **recurring USD obligation** is more informative than the LBP line.

Operationally, a school requiring a recurring USD payment for secondary is signaling that its parent base can access dollars and can allocate them to education, which is the closest observable analogue to paying international tuition.

## 3.3 Evidence standards and audit approach (how quality was controlled)
To keep the list usable for real outreach, this report applies a strict evidence hierarchy:

- **Preferred evidence (highest confidence):** Official school **fee PDFs/pages/images** that show:
  - the academic year, and
  - the secondary level, and
  - the USD annual amount and label
- **Accepted supporting evidence:** Official school pages confirming program/grade coverage (for example IB DP pages or admissions pages describing terminal grades), when the fee document itself does not repeat the secondary grade labels.
- **Not accepted for inclusion:** Third-party fee claims, reposts, and non-school domains that cannot be traced back to the school’s own official site.

Where earlier data points existed but could not be supported by on-domain evidence, the school was either excluded from the main list or flagged as a lead requiring verification. This is why Section 2 includes an “Appendix (Limited-Use Leads)” and explicitly notes when a fee table is undated or when campus linkage needs confirmation.

## 3.4 City stop rule (diminishing returns) and how “comprehensive” is interpreted here
The user request is to identify “all” high schools that are expensive enough to plausibly feed \$10,000 – \$15,000+ annual university tuition decisions, while also using judgement about where to stop adding cities.

In practice, “comprehensive” can be delivered in two layers:

1) **Comprehensive coverage of the high-yield universe:**  
Beirut and the Mount Lebanon belt contain the highest density of schools with published, recurring USD secondary requirements at levels that strongly correlate with international tuition affordability. This area is where the majority of the addressable volume is concentrated.

2) **Evidence-based extension to other regions until returns become negligible:**  
Outside Beirut and Mount Lebanon, fewer schools publish auditable annual USD secondary fees, and where published, USD portions often cluster in the low thousands and may be weaker predictors of sustained \$10,000 – \$27,000 annual university spending. The report therefore adds non-Beirut cities only where the criteria are met with primary evidence.

**Stopping rule used:** once additional cities yield **few or no new schools** that meet the hard criteria (secondary confirmation plus annual USD requirement from official sources), the report stops expanding geography and instead documents “negative findings” and data gaps. This preserves list reliability and keeps the targeting set actionable.

## 3.5 Important caveats (what the list does and does not guarantee)
- **Not all schools publish fees publicly.** Some schools likely charge meaningful USD components but share fee grids only through parent portals or internal circulars. Those schools cannot be included under the evidence rules until documents are obtained.
- **Year labeling varies.** Some schools publish fee tables without stating the academic year on the table itself. Those entries are flagged as lower confidence and should be verified before outreach campaigns.
- **Campus specificity matters.** Networks can have multiple campuses with different fee policies. When campus-level confirmation is not explicit on the fee evidence, the entry is flagged for follow-up.
- **USD amount alone is not the whole affordability story.** Household capacity depends on family size, competing education costs, and the intended degree (medicine vs non-medicine). The USD fee signal is a practical targeting proxy, not a guarantee of ability or willingness to pay.

## 3.6 Recommended next steps (to make this list fully operational for recruitment)
1) **Convert the list into a CRM-ready master table** with consistent city naming, campus routing, counselor contact fields, and an “evidence link” column for quick internal verification.
2) **Close the biggest remaining gaps via structured outreach** to admissions/accounting offices of schools that are high-reputation feeders but do not publish annual USD components. A single request often resolves the missing piece: “Please share the current secondary fee schedule showing the USD portion (support/operating/solidarity) for Grades 10–12 or Seconde–Terminale.”
3) **Run an annual refresh cycle each spring/summer** (typically when new tuition circulars are released), because USD components and payment mechanics can change year to year.
4) **Segment outreach by affordability tier and program interest**:
   - Tier 1 schools: prioritize medicine and Athens pricing prospects; focus on outcomes, accreditation, and career pathway.
   - Tier 2 schools: prioritize Nicosia pricing and strong scholarship narratives.
   - Borderline schools: target high-achievers with merit pathways and early financial planning conversations.

This approach ensures the final report is both strategically comprehensive (capturing the true high-yield universe) and operationally reliable (grounded in verifiable USD annual fee signals for secondary students).
