# Affordability and economics

## Affordability signals for Lebanese students
- Currency volatility (LBP vs USD) makes families seek **USD/EUR-denominated price guarantees** and predictable installment schedules.
- Remittances from relatives abroad often fund tuition; provide clear bank transfer instructions and proof-of-funds templates for Greece/Cyprus visa/residence steps.
- Cost-of-living sensitivity is high; Athens is typically positioned as slightly lower cost than Nicosia, but both must be benchmarked against France/Italy/Germany and regional hubs (e.g., Dubai).
- Erasmus+/exchange stipends are useful offsets but not core financing; highlight availability after first-year progress.

## Financing norms
- Local education loans are limited; students mix family savings, remittances, and part-time work where allowed. Outline any UNIC payment plan options early.
- Parents value stackable, transparent support (merit + sibling/family + early-payment incentives) with explicit caps to avoid surprises.
- Payment schedules that align with remittance cycles or summer earnings reduce drop-off risk after deposit.

## Positioning implications for UNIC vs UNIC Athens
- Publish Lebanon-facing merit grids (10–30% ranges) and clarify how EU vs non-EU tuition applies to Lebanese passport holders.
- Provide proof-of-funds checklists for Athens and Nicosia visa/residence cases; pair with sample budgets for Beirut/Tripoli/Saida families.
- Use Athens when the cost-of-living narrative matters most; lean on Nicosia for established campus/student services. Keep scholarship terms identical across campuses to minimize confusion.
