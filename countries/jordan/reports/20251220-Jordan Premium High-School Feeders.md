# Jordan Premium High-School Feeders

## Produced by: UNIC Evolve, December 20, 2025

## 1) Executive Summary (Jordan, Premium High-School Feeders for UNIC \$10,000–\$27,000 Tuition)

### Market reality: the “ability-to-pay” high-school universe in Jordan is overwhelmingly Amman-centric
Jordan’s high-fee, internationally oriented upper-secondary market is concentrated in **Amman’s West Amman private-school corridor** (Khalda, Dabouq, Abdoun, Deir Ghbar, Tla’ Al Ali, Sweileh, Airport Road catchment, Naour). This is where families most consistently pay for **IB DP, A-Levels, AP**, intensive English delivery, and university counseling, which are the strongest school-level signals that students can plausibly afford:

- **UNIC Nicosia**: \$10,000 per year (and about \$22,000 per year for Medicine)  
- **UNIC Athens**: \$15,000 per year (and about \$27,000 per year for Medicine)

Outside Amman, premium international upper-secondary options exist but appear **thin and lower-volume**, with fewer schools publishing high fee bands comparable to West Amman.

### What “expensive enough” means in this report (practical, recruitment-oriented)
For recruitment purposes, a Jordanian high school is treated as “expensive enough” when at least one of the following is true:

1. **Published upper-secondary tuition is in a clearly premium band** (typically mid-to-high four figures in JOD, and especially above roughly **9,000 JOD** per year), and/or includes large mandatory add-ons (capital fees, enrollment fees).  
2. The school offers **IB DP, A-Levels, or AP** in upper secondary and is positioned as an international/private elite provider (even if tuition is not publicly posted).  
3. The school is a known **expat and upper-income Jordanian** provider, with structures that commonly correlate with overseas university progression (English-medium instruction, external exams, counseling).

In practice, the schools with the strongest fit for **Medicine affordability** are those with **very high Grade 11–12 tuition and/or large capital fees**, because they better proxy the step-up from general tuition capacity to medicine-level tuition capacity.

## Highest-priority conclusions for UNIC outreach

### 1) Tier 1: Best immediate targets (highest ability-to-pay signal; strongest medicine pipeline potential)
These schools show the clearest alignment with UNIC’s tuition levels, especially for Medicine, based on **published 2025–2026 fees** and program type:

- **King’s Academy (Madaba Governorate, but functions as an Amman-market feeder)**: Upper School tuition published for 2025–2026 with very high day/boarding price points. This is the single strongest “ability-to-pay” signal in the dataset.  
- **American Community School Amman (ACS)**: Published 2025–2026 high school tuition in USD, plus a very large capital assessment fee structure, indicating a distinctly high-income segment.  
- **International Community School (ICS)**: Published 2025–2026 A-Level Sixth Form tuition near the top of the Amman day-school market.  
- **Amman Baccalaureate School (ABS)**: Published 2025–2026 IB DP tuition in the premium band (Grade 11 and Grade 12).  
- **The International Academy – Amman (IAA)**: Confirmed IB World School; fees appear to be presented via an official calculator mechanism rather than a static schedule, so this is a priority “fees verification” target, but the market positioning and program type remain Tier 1.  
- **New English School (NES)**: Published 2025–2026 Sixth Form tuition (Year 12 and Year 13) in a high band, with A-Levels and AP referenced.  
- **Modern American School (MAS)** and **Amman Academy (Nord Anglia)**: Published 2025–2026 IB DP tuition in the premium band.

**UNIC implication:** If UNIC needs a concentrated set of schools likely to produce students who can pay **\$22,000–\$27,000 per year for Medicine**, the Tier 1 list above should be the first wave for counselor engagement, parent events, medicine-focused seminars, and alumni panels.

### 2) Tier 2: Strong feeder schools for general programs, and secondary for Medicine
These schools remain valuable because they offer internationally benchmarked curricula and publish mid-to-high fees, but they are a more mixed signal for Medicine-level affordability:

- **International School of Choueifat (SABIS), Amman**: Published official 2025–2026 fee schedule with Grade 11–12 tuition in the upper band.  
- **Modern Montessori School (MMS)**: Published official 2025–2026 tuition showing IB DP Grade 11–12 pricing in a high band.  
- **Amman National School (ANS)**: Published official 2025–2026 IB DP fees at a mid-premium level.  
- **American International School (AIS)** and **Lycée Français International d’Amman (LFiA)**: Published upper-secondary fees (latest available), credible international pathways, generally stronger for non-medicine program recruitment unless additional household affordability signals are present.  
- **Mashrek International School, Ahliyyah & Mutran School, British International Academy, Cambridge High School (Amman)**: Upper secondary and/or IB DP presence is confirmed for several of these, but **publicly accessible Grade 11–12 tuition could not be recovered** in the latest evidence pass. They should be treated as Tier 2 “verify fees” targets rather than excluded.

**UNIC implication:** Tier 2 schools can generate meaningful volume for **\$10,000–\$15,000 programs**, and can still produce Medicine candidates, but Medicine conversion will likely require sharper screening (academic profile, family financing readiness, interest in healthcare, and willingness for high annual tuition).

## Outside Amman: what to expect (smaller pools, selective value)
- **King’s Academy (Madaba)** is the standout outside-Amman campus with a top-tier affordability signal and should be treated as a major strategic target despite its geography.  
- **Irbid** and **Aqaba** show limited but real international-upper-secondary presence (for example, A-Level pathways), yet the publicly available fees found outside Amman are generally lower and suggest **smaller high-tuition yield**. These markets are better treated as **supplementary channels**, particularly for academically strong students seeking international programs, rather than core high-affordability pipelines.

---

## Immediate execution priorities (what would make this actionable fast)
1. **Anchor outreach in West Amman**: concentrate counselor relations and events around the Tier 1 and Tier 2 schools above; this is where Jordan’s premium international secondary density is highest.  
2. **Run a “Medicine affordability” playbook**: prioritize schools with the highest published fees and capital assessments first, because they best proxy \$22,000–\$27,000 annual tuition readiness.  
3. **Close the remaining fee-evidence gaps**: for a small set of schools, tuition is not publicly posted or is presented via calculators/blocked pages; a short admissions outreach to request official Grade 11–12 fee schedules will materially improve list defensibility and targeting accuracy.

## 3) Method, Inclusion Criteria, Evidence Standards, and Caveats (How to Use This List Confidently)

### 3.1 Definitions and inclusion rules (what counted as a “high school” and what counted as “expensive enough”)

**What counted as a high school for this report**
A school was included only if it clearly operates an **upper-secondary** program that maps to university entry, including at least one of the following:
- **Grades 11 – 12** (Jordanian or American framing)
- **Year 12 – Year 13** (British sixth-form framing)
- **IB DP1 – DP2**
- **Terminale / French Baccalauréat** (final lycée year)

Schools that are K–12 were included only when they were verified to run the upper-secondary segment.

**What counted as “expensive enough” for UNIC tuition pathways**
Because the user’s affordability target is external tuition (\$10,000 – \$15,000 per year, and \$22,000 – \$27,000 per year for Medicine), this report uses a practical “ability-to-pay” framework suited to Jordan’s private-school market:

- **Primary inclusion pathway (fee-based):** The school publishes Grade 11–12 (or equivalent) tuition that is clearly in the premium band for Jordan (in practice, commonly around **9,000 JOD+ per year** for upper secondary, or an equivalent high-fee structure once mandatory add-ons are included).
- **Secondary inclusion pathway (proxy-based, when fees are not public):** The school does not publish tuition, but it shows multiple high-confidence premium signals that correlate strongly with families who can pay overseas tuition. Examples include:
  - **IB Diploma Programme authorization** or a well-established **A-Level sixth form**
  - A clearly international positioning (English-medium delivery, external exam systems, university counseling)
  - Admissions structures consistent with premium schools (notable enrollment or capital fees, selective admissions, or comparable signals)

This approach is intentionally conservative on geography (focus on where premium supply is dense) but inclusive on premium indicators (so that hidden-fee schools are not missed).

---

### 3.2 Evidence standards (how each school’s fee credibility is classified)

Each school is tagged to indicate how solid the tuition evidence is for Grade 11–12 (or equivalent):

- **A (Official):** Tuition comes from a school-published fee schedule, official tuition page, or an official PDF that clearly lists the upper-secondary amounts.
- **B (Secondary):** Tuition appears in a credible third-party listing (or partial official content exists), but there is no clean, school-published Grade 11–12 tuition table available in the captured materials.
- **C (Not publicly published):** No tuition numbers were publicly recoverable; the school is included only when upper secondary is confirmed and premium status is supported by strong proxies (for example, IB DP authorization plus premium positioning).

**Operational guidance:** Treat **A-tag** schools as immediate outreach targets with high confidence in the affordability signal. Treat **B and C-tag** schools as high-value targets that require a quick verification step (request the latest fee schedule).

---

### 3.3 City stopping logic (why the list concentrates on certain cities)
The user requested a city-organized list with judgment on where to stop due to diminishing returns. The report therefore prioritizes cities where premium, internationally oriented upper secondary is both **dense** and **predictive of overseas tuition capacity**:

- **Amman metro dominates**: it contains the largest concentration of IB, A-Level, AP, and premium private schools.
- **Madaba Governorate is included because of King’s Academy**: physically outside Amman, but it functions as a major premium feeder serving the Amman market.
- **Irbid and Aqaba are included selectively**: credible upper-secondary international pathways exist, but volumes and fee signals appear smaller and more variable.
- **Other cities are not expanded in the core list** when there is insufficient evidence of multiple premium upper-secondary providers, or when schools appear predominantly mid-fee private rather than premium international.

This stopping logic is designed to maximize recruitment yield per unit effort.

---

### 3.4 Known caveats and how to interpret them

**1) Fees change annually and can be track-specific**
Many schools vary pricing by:
- Curriculum track (IB, national program, A-Levels)
- New versus returning students
- One-time enrollment or capital fees
- Transportation, lunch, books, exam registration fees

This report treats the **latest available published year** as the best signal and calls out add-ons where the school publishes them.

**2) Some premium schools do not publish static fees**
Several schools present tuition via:
- Interactive fee calculators
- Parent portals
- Direct inquiry only
- Pages that are difficult to access programmatically (redirect loops, blocked access)

These schools remain important, but should be handled as “verify by admissions” targets.

**3) Currency conversion is approximate**
Where USD equivalents are shown for JOD tuition, they are approximate and intended for quick comparability. Budgeting and segmentation should always rely on the school’s official JOD schedule.

**4) Tuition affordability is necessary, not sufficient**
High school tuition is a strong predictor of ability-to-pay, but it is not the only determinant of willingness and capacity to pay overseas university tuition (family priorities, scholarships, sibling costs, and financing options matter). The list is designed to identify plausible pools, not to guarantee conversion.

---

### 3.5 Recommended “verification queue” (fast actions that materially improve accuracy)
For any school in the list with **B or C evidence**, the highest-leverage next step is to request a fee schedule directly from admissions using a short, standardized request:

- Request: “Please share the **latest annual tuition and compulsory fees** for **Grade 11 and Grade 12** (or DP1/DP2, Year 12/13), including any **one-time registration/capital fees**.”
- Ask whether tuition differs by track (IB DP versus other streams).
- Ask about payment plans and whether fees are quoted in JOD, USD, or both.

This converts “premium inferred” into “premium verified,” strengthening targeting and messaging, especially for Medicine.

---

### 3.6 How to use this list for recruitment (practical segmentation)
To operationalize quickly, segment by two dimensions: **ability-to-pay signal** and **program fit**.

- **Tier 1 (highest ability-to-pay signal):** Prioritize for counselor relationships, parent events, and Medicine-focused outreach. These are typically the highest-fee schools and those with large mandatory add-ons.
- **Tier 2 (strong international pathway, mixed fee visibility):** Prioritize for general programs and targeted Medicine screening. Run “verification queue” to confirm fees and identify top households.
- **Borderline international (lower published fees but international exams):** Use as supplementary channels for academically strong candidates, scholarship candidates, or families explicitly interested in international study, but do not treat as primary affordability pipelines without additional screening.

This approach keeps outreach concentrated where premium density is highest, while still capturing credible international-pathway schools that can produce qualified candidates.
