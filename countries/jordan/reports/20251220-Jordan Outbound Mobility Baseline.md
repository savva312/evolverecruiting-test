([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/node/4789141))

([ec.europa.eu](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/cache/metadata/en/educ_uoe_enr_esms.htm?utm_source=openai))

# 2. Outbound Volume and Destination Baseline (what to trust, what to use, what is missing)

## 2.1 Primary baseline definition (what “outbound” should mean)

- **Authoritative construct (UNESCO UIS):** Jordan “outbound mobility” should be measured as **internationally mobile (degree-mobile) tertiary students** (students who physically cross a border to enrol in tertiary education outside their country of origin). ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))
- **Operational definition of “country of origin”:** UNESCO, OECD and Eurostat align on a preferred hierarchy for defining origin for mobility statistics: **prior education**, then **usual residence**, then **citizenship** as a last resort. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))
- **Where the official outbound totals and destination tables should come from (UIS):** UNESCO UIS indicates these series are available through the UIS student mobility tables under “International student mobility in tertiary education” (inbound, outbound, indicators). ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))

## 2.2 Latest outbound volume for Jordan (status of “latest available”)

- **Target metric for this baseline:** *Outbound internationally mobile students in tertiary education (Jordan as country of origin), total headcount* (latest year available plus prior 3 – 5 years). ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))
- **Current limitation:** a **direct UNESCO UIS extract (time series and latest year total)** has not yet been inserted into this baseline package, so the report should treat any externally quoted “UNESCO” totals as provisional until replaced by UIS tables. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))
- **Expected recency and lag:** UIS education releases typically exhibit lag and back-revisions because outbound mobility is derived from **host-country reporting**, not a single outbound register. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/node/4789139?utm_source=openai))

## 2.3 Top destinations for Jordan (what the baseline should include)

- **Required baseline output (UNESCO UIS, preferred):**
  - A **Top 10 host-country ranking** for Jordan-origin internationally mobile tertiary students for the latest year available.
  - A **3 – 5 year trend** for Jordan totals (and, if possible, for priority hosts). ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))
- **Cyprus and Greece relevance check (mandatory for UNIC, UNIC Athens):**
  - Even if **Cyprus** or **Greece** are outside Jordan’s Top 10 destinations, the baseline should still pull their host counts for the latest year (and ideally the last 3 – 5 years) from the same UIS host matrix, to quantify addressable demand. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))
- **Known reliability issue for destination matrices:** UNESCO UIS explicitly notes that outbound counts can be incomplete where host countries do not report international students by origin comprehensively, or where origin criteria differ, which can distort bilateral destination rankings for smaller corridors. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/node/4789139?utm_source=openai))

## 2.4 EU host-country lens (Eurostat UOE learning mobility, Cyprus and Greece)

Because UNIC and UNIC Athens compete inside the European higher education space, a **host-side EU data lens** is valuable as a cross-check and for Cyprus/Greece-specific visibility.

- **Most relevant Eurostat dataset family (degree mobility, host-side):** Eurostat’s UOE learning mobility dataset **`educ_uoe_mobs02`** covers “mobile students from abroad enrolled by education level, sex and country of origin,” with annual coverage shown for **2012 – 2023** in the disseminated table interface. ([dgs-p.eige.europa.eu](https://dgs-p.eige.europa.eu/data/information/ta_eductrain_parteduc_mobil__educ_uoe_mobs02))
- **Why this matters for Cyprus and Greece:**
  - Host dimension includes **Cyprus (CY)** and **Greece (EL)**. ([dgs-p.eige.europa.eu](https://dgs-p.eige.europa.eu/data/view?code=ta_eductrain_parteduc_mobil__educ_uoe_mobs02))
  - Origin dimension includes **Jordan (JO)**, enabling a Jordan-origin corridor extraction for CY and EL (subject to small-cell flags/suppression). ([dgs-p.eige.europa.eu](https://dgs-p.eige.europa.eu/data/information/ta_eductrain_parteduc_mobil__educ_uoe_mobs02))
- **Key coding for consistent extraction (as exposed in the dataset interface):**
  - ISCED11 tertiary total: **ED5-8** (levels 5–8). ([dgs-p.eige.europa.eu](https://dgs-p.eige.europa.eu/data/information/ta_eductrain_parteduc_mobil__educ_uoe_mobs02))  
  - Sex total: **T**. ([dgs-p.eige.europa.eu](https://dgs-p.eige.europa.eu/data/information/ta_eductrain_parteduc_mobil__educ_uoe_mobs02))  
  - Unit: **NR** (Number). ([dgs-p.eige.europa.eu](https://dgs-p.eige.europa.eu/data/information/ta_eductrain_parteduc_mobil__educ_uoe_mobs02))  

**Interpretation caution (EU lens):**
- Eurostat/UOE “mobile students” is intended to reflect **degree mobility** (aligned conceptually with UIS internationally mobile students), but country practices and flags can affect small origin-host cells, which is especially relevant for Jordan-to-Cyprus/Greece. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))

## 2.5 Data caveats to carry forward (baseline hygiene)

- **Outbound mobility is harder to measure than inbound:** UIS flags that **comprehensive enumeration of outbound mobile students** is difficult without systematic tracking by destination and depends heavily on host-country reporting. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/node/4789139?utm_source=openai))
- **Cross-source comparability risks:**
  - Mobility series (UIS, UOE) are conceptually different from **“foreign students”** series (citizenship-based), which can inflate or misclassify mobility for bilateral analysis. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))
- **Practical consequence for this ticket:** until the **direct UIS outbound series and host ranking** are inserted, the baseline should present destination findings as **indicative** and prioritize rapid completion of the UIS extract as the next step. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))

# 5. Data Limitations, Caveats, and Implications for UNIC and UNIC Athens

## 5.1 Data limitations and caveats (what can distort conclusions)

- **Definition and comparability risk (most important):**
  - UNESCO UIS defines an **internationally mobile student** as someone who **physically crosses a border** to study in a destination country different from their **country of origin**. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))  
  - UNESCO UIS, OECD and Eurostat align on an origin hierarchy for mobility statistics: **prior education**, then **usual residence**, then **citizenship** as last resort. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/en/methodology/communication-and-information?utm_source=openai))  
  - Consequence: “Jordanian nationals abroad” (citizenship-based) is not equivalent to “Jordan-origin degree mobility” (prior-education based). Mixing these without labeling will misstate addressable demand.

- **Outbound measurement is structurally hard (host reporting dependence):**
  - UIS notes that outbound enumeration can be challenging where there is no systematic outbound tracking, and where host countries do not report international students by origin consistently, with additional cross-country comparability issues from varying criteria. ([uis.unesco.org](https://uis.unesco.org/node/4789139?utm_source=openai))  
  - Consequence: destination rankings for smaller corridors (including potential Jordan to Cyprus and Jordan to Greece corridors) can be incomplete or suppressed.

- **Time lag and revisions (expect delays):**
  - Eurostat’s learning mobility data transmission is structured around annual reporting with lag (commonly described as **t+2** in the UOE legal and methodological framework). ([eur-lex.europa.eu](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN-CS/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32013R0912&utm_source=openai))  
  - Consequence: “latest year” often differs by source, and back-revisions are possible.

- **Small-cell suppression and footnotes (especially relevant for CY and EL):**
  - Eurostat’s UOE learning mobility series explicitly notes possible **national deviations** in defining country of origin, documented via footnotes and annex material in metadata. ([ec.europa.eu](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/cache/metadata/en/educ_uoe_enr_esms.htm?utm_source=openai))  
  - Consequence: for Jordan-origin counts into smaller hosts, values may be missing, flagged, or suppressed, and any trend interpretation must treat these as data-quality signals, not true zeros.

## 5.3 Implications for UNIC (Cyprus) and UNIC Athens (Greece)

- **Recruitment sizing and expectations**
  - Cyprus operates in a structurally high inbound-mobility environment, Greece operates in a structurally low inbound-mobility environment (2023 shares of tertiary students from abroad: **Cyprus 22.3%**, **Greece 3.0%**, EU average **8.4%**). ([ec.europa.eu](https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/products-eurostat-news/w/ddn-20250822-1?utm_source=openai))  
  - Implication: Jordan-origin volumes into Cyprus may be more realistically scalable through existing international pipelines, while Greece may require more intensive market development and sharper niche positioning.

- **Level strategy and messaging**
  - Jordan’s U.S. cohort is **postgraduate-leaning** (2023/24: Graduate **1,280** of **2,643**, **48.4%**). ([opendoorsdata.org](https://opendoorsdata.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/OpenDoors_FactSheet_Jordan_2024.pdf))  
  - Implication: lead with **taught Master’s** and specialization pathways for both UNIC and UNIC Athens, with outcome messaging tailored to realistic post-study options and recognition pathways.

- **Program cluster prioritization (with explicit evidence boundaries)**
  - International demand is heavily STEM-oriented in Open Doors reporting (2023/24: **56%** of international students in STEM; leading areas include computer science, engineering, data analytics). ([studyinthestates.dhs.gov](https://studyinthestates.dhs.gov/2024/12/open-doors-report-a-record-high-number-of-international-students?utm_source=openai))  
  - Implication: prioritize clusters where Cyprus and Greece can credibly compete, particularly **computing, data/AI, cybersecurity, and engineering**, while ensuring program language, accreditation, and employability narratives are robust.

- **Positioning against dominant destinations**
  - Studying abroad is frequently linked to employability, skills acquisition, language improvement, and access to international career opportunities, and sometimes migration intentions. ([oecd.org](https://www.oecd.org/en/topics/international-student-mobility.html?utm_source=openai))  
  - Implication: Cyprus and Greece positioning should be framed around value-for-money, specialization, student support, and credible career and further-study pathways, rather than prestige competition alone.
