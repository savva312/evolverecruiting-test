# 1. Executive Summary: Where Serbia’s “UNIC-Affording” High-School Market Concentrates

Serbia has a meaningful pool of families who can plausibly afford international university tuition at the levels you specified (roughly \$10,000 – \$15,000 per year for standard programs, and about \$22,000 in UNIC Nicosia Medicine or \$27,000 in UNIC Athens Medicine). In practical terms, the most reliable way to find these families at scale is to target **secondary schools that already require premium annual fees** (or operate as internationally oriented schools where fees are intentionally shared only during admissions). Based on tuition evidence and international-program signals, the opportunity is **heavily concentrated in Belgrade**, with only a small number of credible non-Belgrade nodes.

## Most decision-relevant conclusions

- **Belgrade is the clear Tier 1 market** for “UNIC-affording” prospects because it concentrates nearly all of Serbia’s international secondary provision (IB, Cambridge, A-level pathways, French lycée, German Gymnasium with Abitur pathway) and most of the schools with **published annual tuition in the high four figures to high five figures**.
- **Outside Belgrade, yield drops sharply**, but it is not zero. Two non-Belgrade cities stand out as the only ones with strong, tuition-linked international secondary options surfaced in the evidence base:
  - **Novi Sad**: Cambridge-pathway secondary with published annual fees around **€7,205** (secondary and pre-university levels).
  - **Jagodina**: Cambridge-pathway secondary with published annual tuition around **€9,600 – €9,800** (2024/2025).
- **Many Serbian families with high ability-to-pay do not self-identify via “wealth data”**, but they do self-identify via education purchasing behavior. Paying **€7,000 – €10,000 per year** for secondary schooling is a strong proxy for capacity to pay at least **\$10,000 per year** for university tuition abroad. Paying **€13,000 – €16,500** (or **\$28,235**) for secondary is an even stronger proxy for **\$15,000 per year programs and the Medicine price points**.
- **Fee transparency varies by school**. Several high-signal international schools explicitly state that tuition is shared via interview, email package, or an admissions meeting. These should remain in the target set even without published numbers because their operating model and curriculum positioning still correlate strongly with higher-income, internationally mobile families.

## What this implies for recruitment prioritization

### Tier 1: Belgrade schools with published “premium” tuition (strongest affordability signal)
These are the highest-confidence feeders because published annual fees already sit at or above the tuition levels you are recruiting for.

- **International School of Belgrade (ISB)**: \$28,235 for Grades 9 – 12 (2025/2026), plus a €3,000 capital fee, this group is the clearest proxy for Medicine affordability.
- **Ruđer Bošković (IB pathway)**: up to €16,500 (DP2 / Grade 13, 2025/2026), strong signal for \$15,000 per year and Medicine.
- **Chartwell International School**: €14,500 for secondary (Years 7 – 13, 2025/2026), strong signal for \$15,000 per year and Medicine.
- **International School (LINK edu Alliance) / Savremena system (Cambridge track)**: published Cambridge secondary tuition, with full-fee tiers around €13,690 (with an “Upis 2026/2027” cycle reference), strong signal for \$15,000 per year and Medicine depending on household priorities and total cost.
- **Košarkaška gimnazija “Koledž”**: packages up to €14,800 (2026/2027), a premium purchasing signal even though it is sports-positioned.
- **École française de Belgrade (French lycée)**: €7,828 for lycée (2025/2026), a strong \$10,000-per-year university affordability proxy, especially for internationally oriented households.
- **Deutsche Schule Belgrad (DSB), Gymnasium**: €7,600 (Sek I, Grades 5 – 9) and €7,800 (Sek II, Grades 10 – 12) for 2025/2026, this is a strong \$10,000-per-year proxy with a distinctly international credential pathway.

### Tier 2: Belgrade international schools with “fees on request” (high-signal, less quantifiable)
These schools are still strategically important because they deliver internationally framed secondary education and typically serve affluent families, but they require a different outreach approach (relationship-led admissions engagement rather than purely price-screening).

- **British International School (Belgrade)**: states that tuition information is provided via email or during an admissions meeting.
- **PRIMA International School of Belgrade**: states its fee schedule is provided during the scheduled interview or via an email package.
- **Brook Hill International (Secondary)**: states that fees are handled via an admissions “Fees agreement.”
- **Gimnazija “Crnjanski” (Belgrade, IB DP)**: clearly delivers IB Diploma Programme, but tuition is described as “contractual” (ugovorna cena) without a published base amount.
- **Westbridge International School**: publishes “Tuition from 416 e” (for 2025/26 messaging) but the billing unit is not clarified in the available evidence, so it remains a high-priority target for direct confirmation.

### Tier 3: Non-Belgrade international tuition signals (small but real)
- **Novi Sad**: one Cambridge-pathway secondary provider with published fees around €7,205 per year (strong \$10,000 proxy). A lower-fee private gymnasium at €1,800 per year exists, but it is a weaker indicator of \$10,000 – \$15,000 university tuition capacity.
- **Jagodina**: International School Crnjanski publishes secondary fees near €10,000 per year (2024/2025), which is a strong affordability proxy despite being outside the typical capital-city concentration.

## Where diminishing returns begins (and why we stop adding cities)
The evidence indicates that **premium, internationally oriented secondary tuition in Serbia clusters in Belgrade**, with only limited spillover to a small number of additional cities. After covering **Belgrade, Novi Sad, and Jagodina**, the expected incremental yield from adding more cities declines quickly because searches and registry-style checks did not surface comparable fee levels or clearly evidenced international secondary delivery in other major hubs (for example Subotica, Niš, Kragujevac, Pančevo, and several additional mid-sized cities). For recruitment efficiency, this suggests a Serbia strategy that is **Belgrade-first**, then selectively **Novi Sad and Jagodina**, then only expanding further if you have a specific reason (for example a partner institution, a confirmed premium private gymnasium with tuition evidence, or alumni referrals in a particular city).

# 3. Method and Rules: How “Expensive Enough” Was Defined, What Was Included, and Key Caveats

## 3.1 Practical definition of “expensive enough” for the UNIC tuition scenario

Your use case is not to map every secondary school in Serbia, it is to identify **schools that concentrate households with plausible capacity to pay**:

- About **\$10,000 per year** (UNIC Nicosia non-Medicine programs)
- About **\$15,000 per year** (UNIC Athens non-Medicine programs)
- About **\$22,000 – \$27,000 per year** (Medicine tuition points)

Because Serbian public secondary education is typically low-cost, the most defensible affordability signal is **premium private or international high school tuition**, supplemented by curriculum and admissions signals that correlate with international university pathways.

Accordingly, a school was treated as “qualifying” if it met at least one of the following:

1. **Published premium tuition at secondary level**  
   Clear fee evidence (official tuition page or official PDF) for secondary education, with annual tuition commonly in the **high four figures or above** (for Serbia, published totals around **€7,000 – €10,000+ per year** are a strong proxy for households able to pay at least \$10,000 per year in university tuition abroad; published tuition around **€13,000 – €16,500** or **\$28,235** is a very strong proxy for \$15,000 per year and often Medicine affordability).

2. **International curriculum at secondary level (even if tuition is “on request”)**  
   Schools delivering internationally recognized secondary pathways, such as **IB Diploma Programme (DP)**, **Cambridge IGCSE and AS/A Levels**, **French lycée**, or **German Gymnasium leading to Abitur**, were included even when the school states fees are shared only via admissions.

3. **Premium “fees-on-request” operating model (international-school positioning)**  
   Some international schools intentionally do not publish tuition, but explicitly confirm that the fee schedule is provided via interview, email package, or admissions meeting. This is a meaningful affordability signal when paired with documented secondary provision and international credentials.

Public schools were not treated as “expensive enough” by tuition. However, a small optional appendix of **public IB DP gymnasiums** was included as a non-tuition indicator of international intent (useful for outreach and scholarships, less useful for a pure ability-to-pay screen).

## 3.2 Evidence standards and source hierarchy (what counted as “proven”)

To keep the list recruitment-grade, the report uses a strict evidence hierarchy:

- **Tier A (highest confidence): official tuition evidence**  
  Official school tuition pages and official PDFs hosted on the school domain or explicitly linked by it (including fee schedules hosted on platforms like Google Drive when the school site links directly to the document).

- **Tier B: official statement that fees are provided via admissions**  
  A clear sentence on an official admissions page stating that tuition is shared by email, interview, meeting, or contract (for example “fees available during the interview” or “fees presented during a meeting”).

- **Tier C: official curriculum proof without fees**  
  Official pages proving secondary delivery of IB DP, Cambridge IGCSE/A Levels, lycée, or Abitur pathway, even if tuition is not visible.

The city-by-city list in Section 2 labels schools implicitly by transparency, published tuition versus “fees on request” or “ambiguous,” so you can prioritize outreach by confidence level.

## 3.3 How the list was built (completeness approach)

The goal was “fully comprehensive” relative to the category of interest, meaning: **all identifiable premium private or international secondary schools in Serbia that plausibly concentrate UNIC-affording households**, not every secondary school.

The build process combined three complementary approaches:

1. **Registry-first enumeration of private secondary institutions**  
   An authoritative baseline was created using Serbia’s official open-data listings of secondary institutions, filtering for **private founder/owner** entries and then validating each candidate against its official website.

2. **Directory triangulation for international curricula**  
   To reduce the risk of missing international schools that might not be obvious in a registry pass or that market under different English and Serbian names, candidates were cross-checked across international curriculum and expat directories, including:
   - IB “Find an IB World School” directory (for IB DP completeness)
   - Cambridge International school locator signals (where available through school disclosures)
   - French and German abroad-school network indicators (lycée and Abitur pathways)
   - Reputable international school listings used by expat communities (as a discovery layer, always verified on official school sites)

3. **Primary-source validation and deduplication**  
   Each school was validated on its own official pages for:
   - Secondary level coverage (years/grades)
   - Curriculum credentials (IB, Cambridge, lycée, Abitur, A Levels)
   - Tuition evidence (fee tables, PDFs, or “fees on request” statements)

Where brand architecture created confusion, entities were deduplicated. A key example is the **Savremena education system** where “International School (LINK edu Alliance)” and “Savremena gimnazija” appear in the same ecosystem and can present overlapping Cambridge tuition messaging.

## 3.4 City stopping rule (diminishing returns)

The user request asked for a city-by-city organization and the use of judgment on when to stop adding cities. The stopping logic was:

- Continue adding cities when we can identify at least one school with **published premium secondary tuition** or **confirmed international secondary pathway**.
- Stop expanding once additional cities repeatedly show:
  - No premium tuition evidence, and
  - No clearly evidenced IB/Cambridge/A-level/lycée/Abitur secondary delivery in private or international schools

This is why the list concentrates on **Belgrade** (largest cluster), then a small number of **non-Belgrade nodes** where qualifying schools with published tuition or strong international-pathway evidence exist (notably **Novi Sad** and **Jagodina** in the current evidence base).

## 3.5 Limitations and how to interpret the results

- **Affordability is probabilistic, not guaranteed.** Even in premium schools, households vary. Some students may be on discounts, scholarships, or corporate and diplomatic support.
- **Public-school students can still be “UNIC-affording.”** This report focuses on high-confidence affordability signals; it will undercount affluent families whose children attend selective public gymnasiums.
- **Tuition changes frequently.** International schools typically update fee schedules annually; some pages refer to enrollment cycles (for example 2025/2026 or 2026/2027). A practical operating approach is to refresh tuition pages each recruitment cycle and capture updated PDFs.
- **“Fees on request” schools require relationship-driven data collection.** For PRIMA, Brook Hill, British International School, and similar institutions, the next step is to request the fee schedule through admissions to convert high-signal targets into quantified affordability bands.

## 3.6 How to operationalize the list for recruitment

For efficient UNIC pipeline development, the recommended sequencing is:

1. **Start with published premium-tuition schools in Belgrade**, prioritize those with tuition at or above the \$15,000 per year proxy band, then those in the \$10,000 per year proxy band.
2. **Add “fees-on-request” international schools in Belgrade** as strategic accounts (counselor relationships, school visits, targeted events).
3. **Expand to the highest-yield non-Belgrade nodes** with published international secondary tuition (Novi Sad, Jagodina).
4. Use the optional public IB DP appendix as an **international-intent channel**, especially when scholarship messaging is part of the strategy.
