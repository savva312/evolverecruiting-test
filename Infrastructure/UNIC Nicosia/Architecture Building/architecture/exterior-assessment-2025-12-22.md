## 1) Scope of this assessment (what “outside” includes here)

Based on the available photos, “outside” coverage includes:
- Street frontage and visitor approach (sidewalk/promenade, banners, wayfinding).
- The main glazed entry and adjacent curtain wall frontage.
- Courtyard/lightwell areas and exterior seating/terraces.
- The exterior steel stair and upper/roof access route.
- Perimeter/boundary conditions (fences, alleys, adjacent industrial edges).
- Service access controls (boom gates), waste staging, and visible external services.
- Rooftop plant and roof edge safety elements that were photographed.

## 2) Stakeholder perspectives (what to look for)

### Architect / designer
- Read the arrival sequence and how transparency/opacity (curtain wall vs translucent panels) signals public vs studio/service zones.
- Treat courtyards, benches, decks, and the sculptural plinth as place-making moments; note where comfort, shade, and accessibility could be improved.
- Track material transitions (pavers → thresholds; metal rails; panel joints) as likely durability/maintenance hotspots.
- Use the set as a baseline for any future campus-edge upgrades (signage hierarchy, lighting, landscape buffers).

### Engineer (MEP / structural / civil)
- Use drainage-related photos to flag falls, trench drains, ponding, and water management at thresholds and stairs.
- For external plant and utilities, capture labels, isolation points, clearances, and weatherproofing; photos should trigger a follow-up nameplate set.
- For stairs/guardrails, treat photos as a screening tool; confirm compliance via measured checks (guard heights, handrail continuity, slip resistance).
- For access-control hardware (boom gates, fences), confirm impact protection and safe maintenance access.

### Facilities / operations
- Map service routes, waste staging, and boundary conditions; prioritize lighting/CCTV coverage in long alleys and perimeter corridors.
- Watch for slip/trip risks (wet pavers, leaf litter, loose furniture) and keep egress widths clear.
- Use condition shots to plan maintenance cycles (cleaning glazing, repainting rails, fixing paver settlement, graffiti removal).

### Marketing / admissions
- Use the entrance, glazed corner, and courtyards as hero story beats; pair with student activity (consent) and interior studio shots in a future set.
- Avoid “service-only” images in recruitment pages; keep them for behind-the-scenes operations or sustainability narratives.
- Use wayfinding/signage photos to improve the visitor journey before filming virtual tours.

### Educator / student experience
- Use arrival and circulation shots to build orientation maps (where to enter, where to gather, where to find studios).
- Treat the building as a living lab: discuss daylight, envelope choices, circulation, and how services are integrated (or exposed).
- Note safety cues (assembly point, egress routes) for onboarding and drills.
